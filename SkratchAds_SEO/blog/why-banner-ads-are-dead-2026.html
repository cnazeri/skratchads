Why Banner Ads Are Dead in 2026
(And What to Do Instead)

March 17, 2026 · 9 min read · By SkratchAds

Banner ads earn $0.50–$1.50 eCPM, get a 0.56% click-through rate, and are actively blocked by 37% of US mobile users. If you're still relying on standard banners to monetize your app, you're not just leaving money on the table — you're slowly degrading the experience that made your users download the app in the first place.

This isn't a hot take. The data has been building for years. But in 2026, the combination of Apple's ATT framework, cookie deprecation, banner blindness at scale, and a new generation of engagement-first ad formats has pushed banner advertising past the point of no return.

Here's the full picture — and what smart publishers are doing instead.

The Numbers Don't Lie: Banner Ads Are Failing Everyone

Let's start with the data, because the data is damning.

The problem isn't your app. It's the format itself.

Banner Blindness Is a Documented Neurological Response

Banner blindness isn't just user preference — it's a trained neurological response. After years of exposure to banner ads in predictable positions, the human brain has literally learned to filter them out before they reach conscious attention. Eye-tracking studies show that users' gaze patterns actively avoid the areas where banners typically appear.

What started as a minor UX complaint in the early 2000s has evolved into a full-scale avoidance behavior that no amount of creative optimization can fix. The format is fundamentally broken — and users know it, even if they can't articulate why.

These are users who disliked ads so much they took deliberate action to remove them entirely. For publishers, every one of these users generates zero ad revenue — regardless of how many impressions they generate.

Three Forces Accelerating Banner Death in 2026

Banner advertising has been declining for years, but three specific forces have accelerated its collapse — and they're not going away.

1. Apple ATT Gutted Targeting Precision

Apple's App Tracking Transparency (ATT) framework, introduced in 2021 and fully enforced through 2023–2024, requires apps to explicitly ask users for permission to track them across apps and websites. The result: opt-in rates sit at roughly 25–30%, meaning advertisers lost targeting data on 70%+ of iOS users almost overnight.

Why does this matter for banners specifically? Because banner ad value is almost entirely dependent on targeting precision. Without behavioral data, a banner is just a generic rectangle being shown to a user who almost certainly doesn't care. The already-low 0.56% CTR is largely sustained by the small fraction of targeted users. As targeting degrades, that number goes lower.

2. Third-Party Cookie Deprecation Finished the Job on Android

Google's phased deprecation of third-party cookies in Chrome — combined with Privacy Sandbox initiatives — replicated the ATT effect on the Android and mobile web side of the ecosystem. Retargeting, cross-site behavioral tracking, and lookalike audience modeling have all taken serious hits.

The result is a mobile advertising ecosystem where behavioral targeting — the primary mechanism that made banner ads worth buying — is no longer reliably available. Advertisers who once paid premium CPMs for precisely targeted banners are reallocating budgets to formats where intent signals come from user behavior within the ad itself, not from third-party data.

3. The Race to the Bottom Is Complete

As banner performance declined, publishers flooded ad networks with more inventory to compensate — more banner placements, more ad refresh cycles, more aggressive positioning. The predictable result: supply far outpaced demand, and eCPMs collapsed.

"Publishers are trapped in a race-to-bottom CPM war. More impressions at lower rates, degrading the user experience that made their app worth monetizing in the first place."

Adding more banner placements to a failing format doesn't fix the format — it just accelerates the degradation of your app while adding marginally to your revenue. It's the publishing equivalent of working harder at something that fundamentally doesn't work.

Interstitials: Higher eCPM, Higher Cost

Interstitials earn $5–8 eCPM — a meaningful improvement over banners. But the tradeoff is session interruption. Full-screen takeovers that appear between content force users to wait or find the close button, creating frustration. Study after study shows interstitials correlate with higher uninstall rates, lower session lengths, and worse app store ratings. You earn more per impression and lose users faster. Net result: often a wash.

Rewarded Video: Strong eCPM, Wrong Placement

Rewarded video is the current gold standard — $15–30 eCPM, voluntary engagement, and users who opt in are genuinely receptive. The problem is placement: rewarded video requires a full-screen interruption and works best at natural breaking points in gameplay or content. It can't simply replace a banner; it requires deliberate placement and the right type of app context. Not every app has natural rewarded video moments.

Rewarded video is also competitive — the inventory is crowded, demand fluctuates significantly, and the format is increasingly familiar to users who now know exactly how to skip to the reward without engaging with the ad content.

Every other format compromises on at least one of these dimensions.

The Scratch-Off Mechanic: Why It Works When Banners Don't

The psychology behind scratch-off lottery tickets is well-documented and powerful. Americans spend over $100 billion annually on scratch-off tickets — voluntarily, knowing the odds are against them, for any chance at a reward. The behavior is driven by a combination of anticipation, curiosity, and the possibility of reward — three psychological triggers that static banner ads simply cannot activate.

SkratchAds applies this mechanic to mobile advertising. A polite, edge-anchored banner loads with an animated scratch cue. Users who choose to engage scratch the surface — a 3–5 second interaction — and reveal either a real prize (win path) or a brand ad (lose path).

The key insight is what happens on the lose path. A user who just spent 3–5 seconds actively scratching is in a fundamentally different psychological state than a user who glanced past a static banner. Their attention is engaged, their curiosity is activated, and they're primed for the brand message that follows. The result: 5–10× the click-through rate of a cold banner impression on the same inventory.

How to Get Started: A Publisher's Action Plan

If you're convinced it's time to move beyond banners, here's a practical starting point:

  1. Audit your current banner revenue. Pull your last 90 days of AdMob or mediation network data. Note your average eCPM, fill rate, and estimated revenue per daily active user. This is your baseline.
  2. Identify your banner placements. Which banners are running where? Which are performing worst? These are your best candidates for replacement or augmentation.
  3. Evaluate format options honestly. Does your app have natural rewarded video moments? If yes, rewarded video should be in your stack regardless. If not, or if you want non-intrusive supplementary revenue, scratch-off banners fill that slot.
  4. Run a POC before committing. Any format you're considering should be tested with real data from your audience before you make a permanent integration decision. SkratchAds offers free 30-day POCs — take us up on it and compare the numbers directly against your banner baseline.
  5. Measure what matters. eCPM is one metric, but also track session length, retention rates, and app store rating before and after any ad format change. Revenue per user over 30 days is a more honest measure of whether a format is helping or hurting your app.

Banner ads served a purpose when mobile advertising was new and targeting was precise. In 2026, neither of those conditions exists. The format has run its course. The publishers who thrive in the next five years will be the ones who made the transition early — before the floor falls further and the user patience runs out entirely.
